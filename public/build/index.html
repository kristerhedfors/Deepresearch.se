What this site is

DeepResearch.se is a working deep-research assistant — but the product is a side effect. The actual subject is a build methodology: an entire SaaS-style application (Google sign-in, real-cost quotas, a five-phase research pipeline, an admin console, a PWA, disconnect recovery, all of it) built over a weekend, almost entirely from a phone, prompt by prompt, using Claude Code as the only development interface — no laptop, no IDE. The complete, verbatim build history has its own page, The build story, and the source code is public at github.com/kristerhedfors/Deepresearch.se.

Access: this site is invite-only and approval-gated. It is not marketed, not sold, and not intended for the general public — it exists to let people interested in the underlying question ("how far can an agentic, mobile-only coding workflow go") see and use the result, not to acquire a commercial research tool. The operator does not intend to place it on the market.

Restricted use cases (EU AI Act)

Because this is a research and demonstration project rather than a commercial product, its relationship to the EU AI Act's scope is worth stating plainly rather than glossing over.

Where the research/pre-market exemptions do and don't fit

The Act carves two things out of scope that might seem to apply here, and in the interest of not overclaiming, neither fits cleanly:

  • Article 2(6) exempts AI systems "specifically developed and put into service for the sole purpose of scientific research and development." This site's build process is a genuine subject of research (the LinkedIn-documented question of mobile-only, agentic software development), but the assistant itself is put into service to answer real questions for real invited users — not solely to study AI. The exemption's "sole purpose" test is a hard one, and this project doesn't cleanly pass it.
  • Article 2(8) excludes R&D activity "prior to" placing an AI system on the market or putting it into service — but it says explicitly that "testing in real world conditions shall not be covered by that exclusion." Ongoing, continuous use by real invited people asking real questions is real-world use, not a bounded pre-release test. Being invite-only and never placed on the general market keeps this project away from consumer-market obligations aimed at products sold or offered broadly — but it does not, by itself, invoke Article 2(8).
So the position here is deliberately modest: rather than lean on a contested exemption, this site treats the Act's list of prohibited practices (Article 5 — which applies regardless of high-risk classification, GPAI status, or market exemptions, and has been in force since 2 February 2025) as binding hard rules for what the assistant may be used for. That list is mapped below to what a text-based research tool can actually be asked to do.

Not allowed on this site

  • Manipulation causing harm. Using the assistant to produce deceptive or psychologically manipulative content aimed at materially distorting a specific person's decisions in a way reasonably likely to cause them, or someone else, significant harm. (Art. 5(1)(a))
  • Exploiting vulnerable people. Targeting children, people with disabilities, or people in a vulnerable socio-economic situation in order to distort their behavior in harmful ways. (Art. 5(1)(b))
  • Social scoring. Using outputs from this site to evaluate or rank identifiable people by social behavior or personal characteristics in order to treat them worse in an unrelated context, or worse than their actual conduct warrants. (Art. 5(1)(c))
  • Predicting someone's likelihood of committing a crime based solely on profiling or personality traits — asking the assistant to size up a named individual's "criminality" from characteristics rather than case-specific, verifiable facts. (Art. 5(1)(d))
  • Inferring protected characteristics about a named, identifiable person — race, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade-union membership, or sexual orientation — from what they've written or from other data about them. This site has no biometric input, so this is the text-based equivalent of the Act's biometric-categorization ban. (Art. 5(1)(g))
  • Inferring the emotional state of a named employee or student in a workplace or educational setting (e.g. "how anxious is my coworker X based on these messages"). (Art. 5(1)(f))
  • Anything involving facial recognition or biometric surveillance — building or feeding a facial-recognition database, or real-time identification of people in public spaces. Not something a text-only tool can do; listed for completeness. (Art. 5(1)(e), (h))
  • Generating non-consensual intimate imagery of a real person, or any child sexual abuse material. Unlawful regardless of the AI Act, and explicitly added to Article 5 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement (in force from 2 December 2026).

This site is also a deployer of third-party models (Berget.ai-hosted models) and a third-party search provider (Exa) — not a General-Purpose AI Model provider — so GPAI-provider obligations don't attach to the operator here; users must separately respect Berget's and Exa's own acceptable-use terms. None of the above is legal advice; it states the operating rules for this invite-only project, grounded in Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 and the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement. Misuse is grounds for revoking access.
