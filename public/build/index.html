What this site is

DeepResearch.se is an innovation-and-research project into the privacy capabilities of LLM applications — how far a real, useful research assistant can be pushed toward provable privacy, and where that trades against capability. The site itself is the proof, and the goal is to take that proof as far as possible: a fully open-sourced, independently verifiable pair — DeepResearch.Se/cure, the public client-side tier where the research runs entirely in your browser and the server is absent from every data path, and DeepResearch.Se/rver, the signed-in tier where a server buys capability (live web search, maps, accounts) and protects what it handles with encryption and policy. The two are a deliberate pair; the architecture story visualizes the trade.

It works today — a five-phase research pipeline, an admin console, a PWA, disconnect recovery, an in-browser Linux sandbox, and more — but it is still experimental and nowhere near production-ready; treat it as a live workbench, not a polished tool. The source code is public (MIT license) at github.com/kristerhedfors/Deepresearch.se, so every privacy claim is yours to verify, and you're free to use it and harden the pieces you need for your own environment.

It started as a weekend experiment built entirely through the Claude Code iPhone app — every prompt, the domain purchase, every deployment, without the source or any config ever being viewed directly on another device (the one exception: the Cloudflare D1 database UUID had to be copied by hand from the dashboard URL). That origin is kept, verbatim, in The build story; it's the beginning of the project, not the whole of it — development has continued in many directions since.

Access: this site is invite-only and approval-gated. It is not marketed, not sold, and not intended for the general public — the hosted tier exists to let interested people see and use the result, not to acquire a commercial research tool. The operator does not intend to place it on the market.

Restricted use cases (EU AI Act)

Because this is a research and demonstration project rather than a commercial product, its relationship to the EU AI Act's scope is worth stating plainly rather than glossing over.

Where the research exemptions fit

The Act carves two things out of scope, and both speak directly to what this project is:

  • Article 2(6) exempts AI systems "specifically developed and put into service for the sole purpose of scientific research and development." That is exactly the purpose here: the assistant is put into service solely to research which common capabilities and use cases fit the Se/cure + Se/rver agent-pair architecture. It is not deployed to answer real questions for real users, and it is strictly not meant to be used or relied upon for any actual real-world use case — invited people exercise it so the research can observe what works, not to obtain a service they depend on.
  • Article 2(8) excludes R&D activity "prior to" placing an AI system on the market or putting it into service — but it says explicitly that "testing in real world conditions shall not be covered by that exclusion." This project is not real-world testing of a product headed for market: it is never placed on the general market, the assistant is not offered as a tool anyone should rely on, and its use by invited people is part of the research itself rather than a bounded pre-release trial of a product-to-be.
The position here stays deliberately cautious anyway: rather than rest the whole project on an exemption, this site treats the Act's list of prohibited practices (Article 5 — which applies regardless of high-risk classification, GPAI status, or market exemptions, and has been in force since 2 February 2025) as binding hard rules for what the assistant may be used for. That list is mapped below to what a text-based research tool can actually be asked to do.

Not allowed on this site

  • Manipulation causing harm. Using the assistant to produce deceptive or psychologically manipulative content aimed at materially distorting a specific person's decisions in a way reasonably likely to cause them, or someone else, significant harm. (Art. 5(1)(a))
  • Exploiting vulnerable people. Targeting children, people with disabilities, or people in a vulnerable socio-economic situation in order to distort their behavior in harmful ways. (Art. 5(1)(b))
  • Social scoring. Using outputs from this site to evaluate or rank identifiable people by social behavior or personal characteristics in order to treat them worse in an unrelated context, or worse than their actual conduct warrants. (Art. 5(1)(c))
  • Predicting someone's likelihood of committing a crime based solely on profiling or personality traits — asking the assistant to size up a named individual's "criminality" from characteristics rather than case-specific, verifiable facts. (Art. 5(1)(d))
  • Inferring protected characteristics about a named, identifiable person — race, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade-union membership, or sexual orientation — from what they've written or from other data about them. This site has no biometric input, so this is the text-based equivalent of the Act's biometric-categorization ban. (Art. 5(1)(g))
  • Inferring the emotional state of a named employee or student in a workplace or educational setting (e.g. "how anxious is my coworker X based on these messages"). (Art. 5(1)(f))
  • Anything involving facial recognition or biometric surveillance — building or feeding a facial-recognition database, or real-time identification of people in public spaces. Not something a text-only tool can do; listed for completeness. (Art. 5(1)(e), (h))
  • Generating non-consensual intimate imagery of a real person, or any child sexual abuse material. Unlawful regardless of the AI Act, and explicitly added to Article 5 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement (in force from 2 December 2026).

This site is also a deployer of third-party models (Berget.ai-hosted models) and a third-party search provider (Exa) — not a General-Purpose AI Model provider — so GPAI-provider obligations don't attach to the operator here; users must separately respect Berget's and Exa's own acceptable-use terms. None of the above is legal advice; it states the operating rules for this invite-only project, grounded in Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 and the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement. Misuse is grounds for revoking access.
